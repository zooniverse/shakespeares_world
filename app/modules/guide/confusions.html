Common Confusions

Category 1: 16th/17th Century forms that are easily confused with one another

MINIM letters: i/u/n/m.
i u n m
When any of these letters are next to each other it can lead to minim confusion! Count the minims (the individual strokes) at their base to determine how many minims are present, and then do some quick math and contextualization. For example, the “in” in “thing” consists of three minims, which could be an “in” or “m” or “ni” or “ui” or “iu.” Context tells you that it is an “in.”

Is it f or a long s?
Both ‘s’ and ‘f’ are double length; ‘f’ has a cross bar.

Is it y, g or q?
y g q
The ‘g’ has a hat across the bowl and a dramatic tail swing to the left; the base of y’s bowl is angular and the tail normally ends with a swing to the right; ‘q’ is just an awkwardly formed letter, usually with a straight descender.

Extra note: ‘special p’

Contractions are often noted with the letter ‘p’, standing for ‘pro’, ‘pre’, ‘par’, ‘per’. This is called a ‘special p’. ‘p’ followed by a vowel and ‘r’ was sometimes shown by a horizontal line through the stem of the ‘p’, or by a left curve from the tail of the ‘p’. ‘p’ followed by ‘r’ and then a vowel was shown in different ways. Writers did not always use ‘special p’ contractions in the same way, so use the context to help you. See following examples:
parliament
perceive
proclamacions
partes
prepare
